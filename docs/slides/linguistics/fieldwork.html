Fieldwork

Levels of analysis

  • phonetics - study of speech sounds as physical entities
  • phonology - study of the organization and function of speech sounds
  • morphology - study of the structure of words
  • syntax - study of the structure of sentences
  • pragmatics - study of how context contributes to meaning
  • text linguistics = study of the structure of utterances bigger than sentences

Phoneme

  • a smallest possible phonetic unit that distinguishes one word from another
  • any set of similar speech sounds that speakers of a language consider to be the same basic sound

Classes of speech sounds

  • vowels - pronounced without a stricture in the vocal tract, forming the nucleus of a syllable
  • consonants - articulated with complete or partial closure of the vocal tract (except for the h sound), forming the onset or coda of a syllable

Vowel

  • median - the air passes along the middle of the tongue
  • oral - at least some of the air must escape through the mouth
  • continuant - produced without a complete closure in the oral cavity
  • frictionless - produced with an open vocal tract without friction in the vocal tract

The most sonorous part of the syllable is typically a vowel.

Vowel articulation

  • position of the jaw
  • position of the tongue
  • position of the lips
  • nasalization
  • length

Vowel modification

  • tone
  • phonation type
  • diphthongs
  • triphthongs
  • ...

Consonant

A consonant is a basic speech sound in which the breath is at least partly obstructed and which can be combined with a vowel to form a syllable.

The less sonorous margins of a syllable (called the onset and coda) are typically consonants.

Consonant articulation

  • manner - how air escapes from the vocal tract
  • place - where in the vocal tract the obstruction occurs
  • phonation - how the vocal cords vibrate
  • voice onset time - the timing of the phonation
  • airstream mechanism - how the air movement is powered
  • length - how long the obstruction lasts

Consonant modification

  • aspiration
  • palatalization
  • labialization
  • ...

Syllable

A syllable is a basic unit of organization within a sequence of speech sounds.

The structure of a syllable (σ) is: onset (ω) + rhyme (ρ)

The structure of a rhyme (ρ) is: nucleus (ν) + coda (κ)

Morpheme

A morpheme is the smallest meaningful unit within a word.

Word

  • expresses a semantic concept
  • a minimal form that can stand alone
  • has stable sequence of constituents that cannot be rearranged or separated by other words
  • can be divided or modified by morphological rules
  • indivisible for syntactic rules; can be relocated to a new position in the phrase or clause, but not split into parts by syntax

Lexeme

A lexeme is a basic abstract unit of meaning; it consists of all forms of a word related through inflection.

  • belongs to a particular syntactic category
  • has a certain semantic value (meaning)
  • has a corresponding inflectional paradigm

Lemma

A lemma is a canonical citation form of a lexeme, chosen by convention.

Inflection/derivation

  • inflectional rules relate a lexeme to its forms
  • derivational rules relate a lexeme to another lexeme

Lexemes are grouped into syntactic categories based on:

  • morphological properties
  • distributional properties

Syntactic category

  • lexical categories (open classes)
  • functional categories (closed classes)

Grammatical category

A grammatical category (grammatical feature) is a property of items within the grammar of a language.

Within each category there are two or more possible values (called grammemes), which are normally mutually exclusive.

Syntactic categories should not be confused with grammatical categories.

Syntactic and grammatical categories are language specific.

Names of categories are arbitrary.

Constituent

A constituent is a word or a group of words that function as a single unit within a hierarchical structure.

Phrase

  • a constituent built around a head lexical item and working as a unit within a sentence
  • the head identifies the type and linguistic features of the phrase
  • can contain a clause, but is missing subject or predicate or predicands

Clause

  • a constituent that comprises a subject (expressed or not) and a predicate
  • a part of a sentence that can function independently as "a complete thought" (?)
  • may be independent (stand by itself as a complete sentence) or dependent (cannot stand as a complete sentence)

Sentence

  • a biggest constituent that can be analysed with syntax rules
  • a clause composed from phrases and subclauses that realizes a function of a sentence ("expresses a complete thought")

Classification of sentences

  • By clause structure: simple, compound, complex, compound-complex
  • By function: declarative, interrogative, imperative, exclamatory
  • By completeness: major (subject + predicate) and minor (missing a main clause)
